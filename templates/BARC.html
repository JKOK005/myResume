The BARC project

Berkeley Autonomous Race Car

Vehicle dynamics 131

The BARC project is a University of California, Berkeley initiative to further research in the area of vehicle dynamics and controls. It features a water-jet crafted vehicle steel frame, wheel suspension and protective bumpers which are really handy for frequent crashes.

The vehicle handles wifi communication by having its own router. The Odroid unit acts as the brain which distributes commands to the Arduino nano for low level vehicle maneuvers. To perform accurate state tracking, we mounted accelerometers and gyroscopes to gather data on accelerations and angular velocities.

Traction control in action

Our group decided to implement traction control as part of our project on the BARC. Traction control involves controlling the slip-ratio experienced by the vehicle to derive maximum tire force. This is achieved by measuring the vehicle's actual velocity (by integrating the accelerometer reading) and measuring the true angular velocity of the wheels using encoders.

The user first defines an engine torque for the vehicle. If the current slip-ratio is smaller than the desired slip ratio, we maintain the engine torque, allowing the vehicle to accelerate. Traction control kicks in when the current slip ratio is larger than desired. The vehicle drops her speed to the desired slip-ratio using a PID loop.

My contribution was to develop the traction control algorithm and interfacing the vehicle with the ROS framework. ROS was used to coordinate message passing between the high level Odroid unit and the low level arduino controls.

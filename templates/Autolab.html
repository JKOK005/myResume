One year of research in surgical robotics

The future of surgery lies in the hands of robots. I am privileged to have worked along well reknowned roboticist Ken Goldberg and highly competent members in the AutoLab.

Throughout the year, I worked on projects involving low level controls of the da Vinci Research Kit (dVRK), OpenRAVE motion planning and Guided Policy Search

Guided Policy Search in MuJoCo

Guided Policy Search is a technique used to filter out optmial policies that the robot uses in an environment where the dynamics is unknown. We model the dynamics as linear and pick a balance between generating guiding samples and policy comparison until we converge to a suitable policy.

My work involves quantifying the sensitivity of different GPS hyperparameters to the overall performance of the robot. Hyperparameter tuning of constants is important in order to ensure the best possible results for an experiment.

Motion planning with OpenRAVE

Motion planning is key in the process of exploring the work cell. The objective here is to bring an arm end effector from its start to target position without obstacle or self collision. The arms was modelled akin to the dVRK with 3 degrees of freedom and simulation was done purely in the OpenRAVE engine.

Algorithms from the Open Motion Planning Library (OMPL) were used to initialize a collision-free trajectory. This trajectory was then optimized using TrajOpt and encapsulating user defined constraints such as joint distance and velocity penalties.

Thus, my contribution to the lab was to develop a high level motion planner to determine an optimal trajectory before performing autonomous controls on the physical dVRK.

SLERP interpolation for dVRK controls

I designed a linear interpolator for the dVRK arms as my first project. The interpolator discretizes start and goal positions into multiple waypoints and applies quaternion SLERP interpolation using supporting a supporting tfx library.

Each intermediate waypoint is passed as a ROS tf message structure and broadcasted in the ROS network for the dVRK arm driver nodes to listen and execute movement.

Speed control was thereafter implemented by varying the time delay inbetween traversing waypoints, allowing the dVRK controls to be more robust.
